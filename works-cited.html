Works Cited

1. Miller, April. “Will Full-Body VR like ‘Sword Art Online’ Ever Be Possible?” AR Insider, 30 Nov. 2021, arinsider.co.

Miller touches on the potential of SAO-level full-dive VR. She addresses developments in VR hardware, neural interfaces, and the concept of immersion. While technological developments are impressive, she does not feel that true sensory replication is within reach yet. Neuralink and BCI development are noted, showing how they can come into play. This article is essential to Position #1, considering the technological developments that are taking us toward an actual SAO experience. It provides a realistic split between current capabilities and the realm of fantasy and provides a true timeline for attaining full-dive VR.

2. Turns, John. “How Close Are We to Full-Dive VR? A Comprehensive Look.” Seisan Software Development | Where Innovation Meets Execution, 24 Oct. 2024, seisan.com.

Turns gives an in-depth breakdown of existing VR and neural interface technology. The article measures the gap between existing systems and real "full-dive" experiences like SAO. It breaks down BCI development, haptic feedback, and possibilities for the future. Turns concludes that although progress is rapid, full sensory immersion remains decades away. The article is ideal for Position #1, founded on research and expert testimony. It also offers a balanced representation of the potential and risk of this emerging technology, which makes it highly relevant to the discussion.

3. Gault, Matthew, et al. “Palmer Luckey Made a VR Headset That Kills the User If They Die in the Game.” VICE, 9 Aug. 2024, vice.com.

Tech journalist Matthew Gault reports on Oculus founder Palmer Luckey's development of a VR headset that will kill the player upon in-game death, which was inspired by SAO. The article explains that the gadget is artwork and commentary as opposed to a commercial product but served to create a huge public outcry. It is an enlightening window to look at the way that concept of "lethal VR" comes into open discussion and reveals how society would react if technology like that ever came seriously to be created.

4. Terbeck, Sylvia, et al. “Physical Presence during Moral Action in Immersive Virtual Reality.” International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health, U.S. National Library of Medicine, 29 July 2021, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov.

This peer-reviewed study looks at how people make moral decisions when interacting in virtual reality worlds. It found that other people being present in VR increases empathy and morality, and that players may be less likely to act violently when avatars are more realistic. This disproves the idea that everyone would become ruthless in a SAO world and suggests that immersive worlds could actually enforce moral instincts. This is a strong argument for Position #2 (Ethics and Morality), showing that realism in VR can increase—not reduce—moral sensitivity.

5. Jern, Alan. “The Psychology behind ‘Squid Game.’” Edited by Ekua Hagan, Psychology Today, Sussex Publishers, psychologytoday.com. Accessed 21 Apr. 2025.

Cognitive scientist Alan Jern breaks down the hit TV show Squid Game to illustrate psychological concepts in life-or-death games. He describes how high pressure degrades performance (Yerkes-Dodson law) and how anonymity (anonymous, uniformed guards) causes individuals to behave in ways they would otherwise deem immoral, as a result of deindividuation and imposition of group norms. Although it is about a television show, the observations are what might happen in a real SAO scenario: ordinary people acting in fatal situations, sometimes cooperating, other times rationalizing brutality. This resource is helpful for its straightforward explanation of moral psychology in extreme situations so that readers can apply theoretical concepts to a situation similar to SAO.

6. Margolis, Ora. “Sword Art Online: Exploring the Metaverse and Visual Reality.” Sabukaru, 26 July 2022, sabukaru.online.

This online article compares Sword Art Online in the context of real-world metaverse advancement, uniting pop culture commentary with tech criticism. It argues that SAO's story "betrays all concepts of an effective, democratic government" by assuming leaders would allow for thousands to remain trapped in VR – something that the author deems improbable in real life. The article highlights that the piece emphasizes how, after a catastrophe of such scale (3,800+ deaths in the SAO plot), trust in VR would be lost and regulators would definitely halt such technology. The article also refers to society's present distrust of new technology (AI, Web3, etc.) and how a disaster like SAO would cement it. This source is useful to learn the government and society response aspect because it contrasts SAO's dramatized crisis management with what would be the case in reality.
